Efforts of the NAACP

By the early 1950s, NAACP was actively challenging segregation laws in public schools, filing lawsuits on behalf of plaintiffs in South Carolina, Virginia, and Delaware.

Facts

The laws of Kansas permitted (but did not mandate) cities with a population of more than 15,000 to establish racially segregated schools based on race. Based on such legal provisions, the Board of Education of Topeka established segregated public primary and secondary schools. However, many public schools in other nearby communities in Kansas at that time did not have such a system of establishing segregated schools.

Linda Brown, an African American primary school student in Kansas, was rejected from attending the white school (Sumner Elementary School) that was clearly close to her home. Instead, she had to take a bus every day to commute to a distant black school.

In the autumn of 1951, with the assistance of the NAACP, the girl's father, Oliver Brown, as the first plaintiff, joined forces with more than a dozen other parents who were also students of this black school to file a class-action lawsuit (é›†ä½“è¯‰è®¼) against the Board of Education of Topeka, demanding that the school district stop its policy of racial segregation. They contended that racially segregated schools had violated Linda Brown's right to equal protection guaranteed by the Fourteenth Amendment of the Constitution.

Procedural History

The plaintiffs filed their cases in federal district courts in various states. In several of these cases, three-judge federal district courts applied the "separate but equal" doctrine established in Plessy v. Ferguson and denied relief to the plaintiffs. Those decisions were appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court, which agreed to hear the consolidated cases.

Argument was heard in the 1952 Term, and reargument was heard this Term on certain questions propounded by the Court.

Issue

The issue presented to the Court was whether racial segregation in public education violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, even when the physical facilities and services are â€œsubstantially equalâ€ under the doctrine of "separate but equal".

Reasoning

1. Factual Level: Racial segregation in public schools results in actual inequality. There are significant gaps in teaching staff, facilities, resources, and other aspects between Black and white schools, which is not "separate but equal."

2. Psychological Harm: Mandatory racial segregation causes African American schoolchildren to develop a sense of inferiority, undermines their learning motivation and mental health, and this psychological harm inflicts irreversible deprivation of educational opportunities.

3. Legal Interpretation: The core of the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution is to ensure that citizens are not subjected to discriminatory treatment based on race. Public education is a fundamental right for citizens' development, and racial segregation directly violates the legislative spirit of this clause.

4. Precedent Overturning: The "separate but equal" principle established in the previous case Plessy v. Ferguson is inapplicable in the field of public education. Since it cannot achieve true equality and conflicts with the core value of equal protection under the Constitution, it should be overruled.
